The Family Conversation

Talking about who will actually pay

Hi, I'm Sarah. NYU Class of 2023, marketing professional. I'm navigating $80k in Parent PLUS loans with my family. The guilt is real, the conversations are hard, but I'm here to help you understand what you're getting into.

The Elephant in the Room

Parent PLUS loans create a unique problem:

  • Legally, the parent is responsible
  • Emotionally, many parents expect the student to help
  • Practically, the student may or may not be able to contribute

And most families never talk about it explicitly.

The Unspoken Assumption

Many parents take out PLUS loans thinking, "My child will help pay this back once they get a job."

Many students assume, "My parents are handling this; it's not my responsibility."

Both can be true at the same time. And that's a problem.

Before You Borrow: Questions to Discuss

For Parents to Ask Themselves

  • Can we afford the monthly payment without help from our child?
  • Will this jeopardize our retirement?
  • What happens if our child can't find a job after graduation?
  • What happens if our child chooses a low-paying career?
  • Are we comfortable with the legal responsibility being ours alone?

For Students to Ask Themselves

  • Do I understand how much my parents are borrowing?
  • Do I know what the monthly payment will be?
  • Am I choosing a major/career that will allow me to help repay this?
  • What if I can't afford to help after graduation?
  • Am I okay with my parents taking on this debt for me?

How to Have the Conversation

1. Start Early

Have this conversation before taking out the loans, not after graduation when payments start.

2. Be Specific About Numbers

Don't talk in vague terms. Use real numbers:

  • "We're borrowing $40,000 total over four years."
  • "The monthly payment will be about $485."
  • "We'll pay $18,000 in interest over 10 years."

3. Discuss Expectations Explicitly

Don't assume. Ask directly:

  • "Do you expect me to help pay this back?"
  • "How much do you expect me to contribute?"
  • "When do you expect me to start contributing?"

4. Consider Different Scenarios

What if:

  • The student can't find a job right away?
  • The student goes to grad school?
  • The student chooses a low-paying career they're passionate about?
  • The parent loses their job or faces a health crisis?

Common Arrangements

Option 1: Parent Pays Entirely

The parent treats this as a gift to the student. No expectation of repayment.

Pros: Clear expectations, no guilt for student

Cons: Can strain parent's finances, especially near retirement

Option 2: Student Pays Entirely

The parent takes out the loan, but the student makes all payments.

Pros: Student takes responsibility, parent's credit isn't damaged by missed payments

Cons: Student has no legal obligation; if they stop paying, parent is stuck

Option 3: Shared Responsibility

Parent and student split the payment, or parent pays while student is in school, student takes over after graduation.

Pros: Distributes burden, teaches student responsibility

Cons: Requires ongoing communication and trust

Option 4: Conditional Arrangement

Parent pays unless student's income exceeds a certain threshold, then student contributes.

Pros: Flexible, accounts for student's actual financial situation

Cons: Requires ongoing negotiation

Putting It in Writing

Consider creating a simple written agreement that outlines:

  • Total amount borrowed
  • Expected monthly payment
  • Who will pay what percentage
  • When student contributions will start
  • What happens if circumstances change

Not Legally Binding, But Still Valuable

A written agreement between parent and student isn't legally enforceable (the loan is in the parent's name), but it creates clarity and accountability.

What If You're Already in Repayment?

It's not too late to have this conversation.

For Parents

  • Be honest about whether you can afford the payments
  • Don't suffer in silence to avoid burdening your child
  • Propose a specific arrangement

For Students

  • Ask your parents directly if they need help
  • Offer what you can realistically afford
  • Don't promise more than you can deliver

When the Conversation Goes Wrong

Sometimes families can't agree. If that happens:

  • Consider family counseling or financial mediation
  • Explore refinancing options that transfer the loan to the student's name
  • Look into income-driven repayment to reduce parent's monthly burden
  • Accept that the relationship may be strained and focus on finding a workable solution

The Bottom Line

Parent PLUS loans are a tool, not a solution. They work best when:

  • Both parties understand the terms
  • Expectations are explicit
  • There's a plan for repayment
  • The family can afford it without sacrificing other financial goals

parent plus loans and the guilt that comes with them

I know this conversation is hard. I've been there. Let me share what I've learned about navigating Parent PLUS loans and family expectations.

My story: I graduated from NYU in 2023 with my dream degree in marketing. I also graduated with $80,000 in Parent PLUS loans that my parents took out in their names. Loans I didn't fully understand until after graduation.

Now I'm 22, working in marketing, and helping my parents make payments on loans that are legally theirs but feel morally mine. The guilt is real. The conversations are awkward. And nobody prepared me for this.

So let me help prepare you.

what parent plus loans actually are

Here's what I wish someone had explained to me when I was 18:

Parent PLUS loans are federal loans that your parents take out in their name to help pay for your education. The key word here is "their." These loans:

  • Are in your parent's name only
  • Show up on your parent's credit report, not yours
  • Are your parent's legal responsibility to repay
  • Have higher interest rates than student loans (currently around 8%)
  • Start accruing interest immediately - no grace period

But here's the complicated part: even though they're legally your parent's loans, many families expect the student to help pay them back. And that expectation? It's usually not discussed clearly until after graduation.

the conversations nobody wants to have

Before college (what I wish we'd done)

I know this is uncomfortable, but you need to have this conversation BEFORE your parents sign anything:

Questions to ask your parents:

  • "How much are you planning to borrow in Parent PLUS loans?"
  • "Do you expect me to help pay these back after graduation?"
  • "If yes, how much and when would payments start?"
  • "What happens if I can't find a job right away?"
  • "Should we put this agreement in writing?"

I know. It feels awful to ask. It feels like you're being ungrateful. But trust me - having this conversation at 18 is way easier than having it at 22 when the bills are due and everyone has different expectations.

After graduation (where I am now)

If you're like me and didn't have that conversation beforehand, you might be navigating this now. Here's what's helped me:

Be honest about your finances. I showed my parents my actual budget - rent, student loan payments, groceries, everything. When they saw the numbers, they understood why I couldn't contribute $800/month like they'd hoped.

Propose what you CAN do. I couldn't afford their full payment, but I could contribute $300/month. We agreed on that, and I've been consistent with it. Consistency matters more than the amount.

Acknowledge their sacrifice. My parents took on significant debt for my education. I make sure they know I appreciate it, even when the conversations about money are hard.

the guilt is real (and it's okay)

Can I be honest? I feel guilty every single day. Guilty that my parents are in debt because of me. Guilty that I can't pay more. Guilty that I chose an expensive school. Guilty that my degree in marketing doesn't pay enough to cover everything.

But here's what my therapist helped me understand: guilt doesn't change the situation, but communication and action do.

If you're feeling guilty about Parent PLUS loans, here's what's helped me:

  • Remember you were 18. You made the best decision you could with the information you had. You're not a bad person for wanting an education.
  • Focus on what you can control. You can't change the past, but you can be responsible now. Make your agreed-upon payments. Communicate clearly. Show up.
  • Set boundaries. You can help your parents without destroying your own financial future. It's okay to say "I can contribute $X but not $Y."
  • Get it in writing. If you're making payments on your parent's loans, document it. Not because you don't trust them, but because clarity helps everyone.

practical advice for managing this

If you're still in school

Have the conversation NOW. Ask your parents:

  • How much they've borrowed so far
  • What the monthly payments will be after graduation
  • What they expect from you

Then run the numbers. Use a loan calculator to see what those payments actually look like. Make decisions about your remaining years with full information.

If you've already graduated

Start the conversation gently. Something like: "I want to talk about the Parent PLUS loans and make sure we're on the same page about expectations."

Come prepared with:

  • Your current budget
  • What you can realistically contribute
  • A proposed timeline
  • Willingness to adjust as your income grows

Real talk: Some parents will say "these are my loans, you don't need to pay them." Others will expect you to cover everything. Most fall somewhere in between. There's no "right" answer - just what works for your family. But you need to know where you stand.

what I'm doing now

I contribute $300/month to my parents' Parent PLUS loans. It's not the full payment, but it's what I can afford while also paying my own student loans and living in NYC.

We have a shared spreadsheet where I can see the loan balance going down. It helps me feel like I'm making progress, even though the number is still terrifyingly high.

I've also committed to increasing my contribution by $50 every time I get a raise. It's not much, but it's something.

And most importantly: I've forgiven myself for being 18 and not understanding what $80,000 in loans really meant. I'm doing the best I can now, and that has to be enough.

final thoughts

Parent PLUS loans are complicated because they mix family dynamics with financial obligations. There's love, sacrifice, expectations, guilt, and a lot of money all tangled together.

I can't tell you what the right arrangement is for your family. But I can tell you that clear communication is better than assumptions, and having hard conversations early is better than having them in crisis mode later.

You're not a bad person for needing help understanding this. You're not ungrateful for asking questions. You're being responsible by trying to figure it out.

And if you're already in the thick of it like me - you're doing better than you think. Keep showing up. Keep communicating. Keep making those payments when you can.

We'll get through this.

What Parents Need to Know

Understanding Parent PLUS loans

What Is a Parent PLUS Loan?

A Parent PLUS loan is a federal student loan that parents take out to help pay for their child's education.

It's not a student loan. It's a parent loan.

How Parent PLUS Loans Are Different

1. The Parent Is the Borrower

The loan is in the parent's name. The parent is legally responsible for repayment.

The student is not a co-signer. The student has no legal obligation to repay it.

2. Higher Interest Rates

Parent PLUS loans have higher interest rates than undergraduate student loans.

  • Parent PLUS: ~8% (as of 2024)
  • Undergraduate Direct Loans: ~5-6%

3. Higher Fees

Parent PLUS loans have an origination fee of ~4% of the loan amount.

If you borrow $10,000, you'll pay a $400 fee upfront, but you still owe $10,000.

4. No Borrowing Limits (Except Cost of Attendance)

Parents can borrow up to the full cost of attendance minus any other financial aid the student receives.

This can be dangerous—there's no cap based on ability to repay.

5. Credit Check Required

Unlike student loans, Parent PLUS loans require a credit check. If the parent has adverse credit history, the loan may be denied.

The Big Difference

Student loans are based on the student's future earning potential. Parent PLUS loans are based on the parent's current financial situation—often when they're nearing retirement.

Who Qualifies?

To qualify for a Parent PLUS loan, the parent must:

  • Be the biological or adoptive parent (or sometimes stepparent)
  • Not have an adverse credit history
  • Have a child enrolled at least half-time in an eligible school

When Do Parents Take Out PLUS Loans?

Common scenarios:

  • The student has maxed out their federal student loan limits
  • The family doesn't qualify for enough need-based aid
  • The parents want to avoid private student loans
  • The student doesn't have credit history for private loans

The Hidden Costs

Interest Accrues Immediately

Unlike subsidized student loans, interest starts accruing the day the loan is disbursed.

There's no grace period. Repayment starts immediately (though you can defer while the student is in school).

No Income-Driven Repayment (Mostly)

Parent PLUS loans don't qualify for most income-driven repayment plans.

The only option is Income-Contingent Repayment (ICR), and only if you consolidate into a Direct Consolidation Loan first.

Limited Forgiveness Options

Parent PLUS loans don't qualify for most loan forgiveness programs.

Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF) is possible, but only after consolidation and 10 years of qualifying payments.

The Emotional Complexity

Parent PLUS loans create a unique dynamic:

  • Parents feel obligated to help their children
  • Students feel guilty that their parents are in debt
  • Families avoid talking about the financial reality
  • Resentment can build on both sides

The Unspoken Expectation

Many parents take out PLUS loans with the informal expectation that the student will help repay them after graduation.

But legally, the student has no obligation. This can create tension if the student can't or won't help.

Should Parents Take Out PLUS Loans?

It depends. Questions to ask:

  • Can we afford the monthly payments?
  • Will this jeopardize our retirement?
  • Are there other options (scholarships, work-study, community college)?
  • Is the degree likely to lead to a job that justifies the debt?
  • Have we had an honest conversation with our child about expectations?

Alternatives to Parent PLUS Loans

  • Student works part-time – Reduces borrowing need
  • Student takes a gap year to save – Delays college but reduces debt
  • Student attends a less expensive school – Community college, in-state public school
  • Private student loans in student's name – Requires credit or co-signer, but shifts legal responsibility
  • Home equity loan – Lower interest rate, but puts home at risk
